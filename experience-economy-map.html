1 CONTENT & IP
Everything in the experience economy is downstream of IP. Without something worth attending, there are no venues, promoters, ticketing platforms, or technology businesses. IP is the scarce input — some assets hold dramatically more value than others, and that gap widens in an attention-scarce world.
Major Sports Leagues
14 cos
Sports franchises are structurally scarce, benefit from media rights inflation, are global in their fandom, and carry deep emotional buy-in. PE ownership rule changes across major leagues and global expansion have fundamentally repriced this asset class. Historically accessible to fewer than 200 people globally, sports ownership is now investable through publicly traded holding companies, specialized PE vehicles, and structured products.

2 PHYSICAL INFRASTRUCTURE
IP is why people show up; physical infrastructure determines how many can show up, what they pay, and what the experience feels like. This is primarily a real estate layer — physical assets in fixed locations with finite capacity. The business model is being rebuilt: legacy venues were designed around seat count and concessions; modern venues are designed around revenue per square foot.
Stadiums & Arenas
11 cos
The backbone of the ecosystem. The US has ~150 major arenas and ~30 NFL-caliber stadiums — finite, location-constrained supply. The average major US arena is 25–30 years old and was designed for a different era. New builds are engineered as technology and hospitality platforms first — suites, biometric entry, AI-driven operations, and mobile commerce built in from the ground up.

3 OPERATORS & DISTRIBUTION
The connective tissue between IP, physical infrastructure, and consumers. Companies here organize events, sell tickets, and help consumers decide what to attend. The model rewards scale and integration — the more touchpoints controlled, the more margin captured at each step. The DOJ antitrust trial against the dominant integrated player (trial began March 2, 2026) is the single biggest variable for this entire layer.
Promoters & Producers
11 cos
Companies that take the financial risk of organizing events. Integrated conglomerates dominate concert promotion and capture margin across the value chain. Festival operators run the highest-margin format — multi-day events with controlled environments for sponsorship, VIP tiers, and on-site revenue. The experience economy extends far beyond concerts: family entertainment, theater, and immersive art each have their own operator ecosystems.

4 SERVICE PROVIDERS
Every other layer — IP holders, venues, operators, technology companies — needs services to function. These are predominantly services businesses — labor-intensive, relationship-driven, and margin-constrained. The layer grows as the experience economy grows, but growing with the market is different from capturing disproportionate value. Exceptions are companies with exclusive rights to marquee events, control over talent pipelines, or scaled platforms spanning multiple service categories.
Talent Representation
8 cos
Agencies represent the artists, athletes, and entertainers that constitute Layer 1's IP, negotiating touring deals, media contracts, endorsements, and brand partnerships. This leverage has increased as touring became the primary revenue source for artists and as athlete brand value expanded through social media and NIL. The challenge: people businesses with key-person risk, opaque economics, and limited scalability.

5 TECHNOLOGY ENABLERS
The digital infrastructure layer — technology that enhances live experiences, improves operational efficiency, or creates new revenue streams. Most is B2B. The layer matters because technology drives the economic transformation in Layers 1–3. The challenge is separating durable platforms from point solutions — and identifying where AI creates genuinely new value versus where it commoditizes existing tools.
Sports Data & Betting Infrastructure
4 cos
The most clearly investable sub-segment in this layer. Sports betting legalization has created a rapidly growing market for real-time data feeds, integrity monitoring, and odds generation — and data providers sit at the toll booth. Every legal bet requires official data, and leagues have granted near-exclusive rights to a small number of providers. This is the sub-segment with the clearest path to durable, scaled businesses; public market access exists.
