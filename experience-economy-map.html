Major Sports Leagues
14 cos

PE ownership rule changes across major leagues and global expansion have fundamentally repriced this asset class. Historically accessible to fewer than 200 people globally, sports ownership is now investable through publicly traded holding companies, specialized PE vehicles, and structured products.

2 PHYSICAL INFRASTRUCTURE
IP is why people show up; physical infrastructure determines how many can show up, what they pay, and what the experience feels like. This is primarily a real estate layer — physical assets in fixed locations with finite capacity. The business model is being rebuilt: legacy venues were designed around seat count and concessions; modern venues are designed around revenue per square foot.
Stadiums & Arenas
11 cos
The backbone of the ecosystem. The US has ~150 major arenas and ~30 NFL-caliber stadiums — finite, location-constrained supply. The average major US arena is 25–30 years old and was designed for a different era. New builds are engineered as technology and hospitality platforms first — suites, biometric entry, AI-driven operations, and mobile commerce built in from the ground up.

Overview

We define the Experience Economy as any business that creates, enables, or monetizes live events that consumers participate in or attend — sports, concerts, festivals, comedy, immersive attractions, hybrid experiences, and the technology and infrastructure that powers them.

This economy is entering a multi-decade expansion driven by structural shifts in consumer behavior, technology, and culture. Experiences and events will become more valuable over time, and the space is set to go through an evolution across how it is produced, distributed, and consumed.

Five Converging Forces
→Contracted Revenue — Major sports leagues (MLB, NFL, NBA) have contracted media revenues for 10+ years. Over 70% of a team's revenue comes from media deals regardless of on-field performance or the economic cycle.
→Growing Wealth Gap — The K-curve economy creates demand for premium/luxury experiences at the top, affordable alternatives in the middle, and budget options for consumers priced out of tier-one events.
→Social Media — A generational shift toward spending on experiences rather than material goods, originally with millennials and Gen Z, is now broadening across age groups.
→Digital Immersion Requires a Counterweight — As AI and technology penetrate daily life, the value of being physically present — in a crowd, feeling something real — grows as a counterweight to digital saturation.
→Physical Scarcity — Attending a live experience requires being in a specific place at a specific time. This is a tangible, scarce resource in a world where almost everything else is abundant and digital.
Layer-by-Layer View
Layer 1 — Content & IP

Everything in the experience economy is downstream of IP. Without something worth attending, there are no venues, promoters, ticketing platforms, or technology businesses. IP is the scarce input — some assets hold dramatically more value than others, and that gap widens in an attention-scarce world. Supply is fixed: you cannot create new NFL franchises, top-tier artists, or cultural institutions at will — but every structural driver pushes demand upward. Distribution is fragmenting while live content consolidates, increasing the IP layer's leverage over distribution. The asset class is being discovered by institutional capital at the beginning, not the end, of a repricing cycle.

Layer 2 — Physical Infrastructure

IP is why people show up; physical infrastructure determines how many can show up, what they pay, and what the experience feels like. This is primarily a real estate layer. The business model is being rebuilt: legacy venues were designed around seat count and concessions; modern venues are designed around revenue per square foot — premium hospitality, sponsorship integration, and technology-first operations. The rebuild cycle is a multi-decade tailwind. Aging venues force construction, every new build generates more revenue per square foot for 30–40 years, and technology permanently changes what this real estate produces.

Layer 3 — Operators & Distribution

The connective tissue between IP, physical infrastructure, and consumers. The model rewards scale and integration — the more touchpoints controlled, the more margin captured at each step. The DOJ antitrust trial against the dominant integrated player (trial began March 2, 2026) is the single biggest variable for this entire layer. A forced separation of ticketing from promotion opens genuine competitive space across all event types; a DOJ loss validates and entrenches the integrated model.

Layer 4 — Service Providers

Every other layer needs services to function. These are predominantly services businesses — labor-intensive, relationship-driven, and margin-constrained. The layer grows as the experience economy grows, but growing with the market is different from capturing disproportionate value. Exceptions matter: companies with exclusive rights to marquee events, control over talent pipelines, or scaled platforms spanning multiple service categories have built real competitive positions.

Layer 5 — Technology Enablers

The digital infrastructure layer. Most is B2B. The challenge is separating durable platforms from point solutions — and identifying where AI creates genuinely new value versus where it commoditizes existing tools. Sports data and betting infrastructure is the exception: clear secular tailwind, limited supply of official data providers, recurring revenue, public market access. The rest requires venture-stage risk tolerance or patience for category consolidation.

Key Risks
Why This Sector Could Stall
⚠Recession Sensitivity — Experiences are discretionary. Demand contracts when consumers are squeezed, and the premium end is tied to corporate budgets and wealth concentration.
⚠VR/AR Substitution — If immersive technology reaches parity with physical attendance, does the scarcity premium erode?
⚠Consumer Fatigue — As supply of events grows, does proliferation dilute the scarcity premium and fatigue the audience?
⚠Rights Escalator Risk — What breaks the media rights escalator? If streaming platforms pull back or advertising enters a prolonged downturn, franchise appreciation stalls.
Open Questions
?Where does content ownership (sports franchise, UFC) versus content infrastructure (Live Nation, ticketing, venues) generate better returns over a multi-decade horizon?
?Does the DOJ break up Live Nation/Ticketmaster? A forced divestiture would be the most significant structural change in this industry in decades.
?Can Sphere and COSM scale beyond their first locations, or are they one-of-a-kind monuments?
?What is the next IP that scales globally? F1 and women's sports moved from niche to institutional in under five years — can newer emerging leagues follow?
?Where does AI create genuinely new markets in live events technology versus commoditizing existing tools and enabling in-house development?
